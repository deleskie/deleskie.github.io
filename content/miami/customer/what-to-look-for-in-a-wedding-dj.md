# What To Look For In A Wedding DJ

## Start With The Job You Actually Need Done

A wedding DJ is part music curator, part MC, part audio operator, part timeline partner, and part room reader. The right choice should make your wedding feel organized, personal, and alive without making the DJ the center of attention.

## Look For Preparation

Strong DJs ask about:

- Ceremony and reception locations.
- Guest count.
- Timeline.
- Planner or venue contact.
- Family names and pronunciations.
- Cultural traditions.
- Must-play and do-not-play music.
- Guest request preferences.
- Outdoor, power, and rain-plan details.

## Look For Clear Communication

You should know what happens after you inquire, after you book, before final planning, and on event day. If communication is vague before payment, it may not improve later.

## Look For MC Judgment

A good MC knows when to speak, how much to say, and how to keep the room focused without sounding forced. Ask how they handle introductions, speeches, family moments, and bilingual announcements if those matter to your guests.

## Look For Ceremony Awareness

If your ceremony is important, ask detailed audio questions. Vows, officiant, readers, wind, speaker placement, and music cues should all be planned.

## Look For Music Flexibility

The DJ should understand your taste, but also understand your room. A strong reception may need Latin music, classics, current hits, family favorites, dinner music, and moments where the energy changes naturally.

## Look For Production Fit

The setup should fit the room. A luxury ballroom, rooftop, hotel, private home, quinceanera, and corporate reception do not all need the same presentation.

## Green Flags

- They ask specific questions before recommending a setup.
- They talk about flow, sound, and guest experience.
- They have a process for music planning.
- They know how to work with planners and venues.
- They can explain what they need from the venue.
- They avoid fake pressure and vague promises.

## Red Flags

- They only talk about playlists.
- They do not ask about ceremony audio.
- They have no pronunciation process.
- They cannot explain backup planning.
- They overpromise without asking about your venue.
- They use generic language that could apply to any event.

## A Simple Test

Ask this: "What would you need to know to make our wedding feel smooth?"

The answer should include sound, timing, family details, music, MC tone, room logistics, and the dance floor.
