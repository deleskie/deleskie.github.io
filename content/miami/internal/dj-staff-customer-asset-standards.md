## Purpose

These notes translate the customer-facing checklists into internal expectations. If we publish an asset that tells customers what matters, our event prep should be able to support it.

## Customer Promise

The public message is:

- Clear sound.
- Polished flow.
- A room that moves.

Every planning call, event packet, and final prep note should connect back to those three outcomes.

## What Staff Should Collect

- Date, venue, room names, and event hours.
- Ceremony location and reception location.
- Guest count.
- Planner, venue, and primary client contacts.
- Timeline draft and final timeline.
- Wedding party names and pronunciation notes.
- Family formality notes.
- Bilingual announcement needs.
- Must-play and do-not-play lists.
- Guest request policy.
- Ceremony music cues.
- Speech/toast order.
- Load-in window, power, parking, and venue restrictions.
- Rain plan or room-flip plan.

## Internal Green Flags

- We ask clarifying questions before assuming the setup.
- We confirm ceremony audio as its own scope.
- We collect pronunciation before event day.
- We know who can approve timeline changes.
- We know which moments are emotionally important.
- We understand the music boundaries before the dance floor opens.

## Internal Red Flags

- No ceremony details.
- No planner or venue contact.
- No final timeline.
- No pronunciation notes for formal introductions.
- No must-play or do-not-play guidance.
- No rain plan for outdoor events.
- No clear load-in or power information.

## Event Packet Minimum

- Client names.
- Venue and room.
- Contact sheet.
- Timeline.
- Ceremony cues.
- Formality order.
- Pronunciation notes.
- Music priorities.
- Do-not-play notes.
- Equipment/setup notes.
- Open questions.

## Tone Standard

Customers should feel guided, not interrogated. Ask direct questions, explain why details matter, and avoid making the planning process feel like homework with no payoff.
